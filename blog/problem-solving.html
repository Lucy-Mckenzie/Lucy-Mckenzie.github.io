Problem solving

Describe a time you were blocked on a simple problem

The problem: I struggled with writing was when writing a function to solve a problem. The function was called the super flip flop in sprint-4. I basically had to grab the information from another part of my code and put it into another function for it to work as I wanted. Being new to the different methods (which are for manipulating codes), I wasn’t aware of the .push() or .map() methods, which could work for solving my problem.

The problem-solving techniques I used: I went over and over it while walking, figuring out if I’d missed anything (turns out I’d missed implementing the push method), I was a little to impatient to solve the problem to take time to reread the brief of what we were building. And after around 20 minutes of problem-solving (trying lots of different variations, almost trying to force my way through), I contacted the foundation's group, and another student replied with his pseudo-code, which had .push() in it.

How did I felt throughout the process and what did I learnt: I felt frustrated and lost in the challenge and should have planned better. It was a good lesson on slowing down and taking time to understand the function. I learned the importance of outlining how the function works and visualizing its parts before coding, instead of blindly searching for the answer.

How do I elegantly solve problems

I don't know how elegant my problem solving skills are at the moment but, I like to rope all my family into what I’ve been working on for the day, my brother knows a bit about coding and understands some concepts, I can explain in more detail to him. While explaining over the table what I’ve been working on I find I can explain it fairly easily by making analogies, and quickly refactoring the parts they don't understand to keep them engaged.

I enjoy visualizing how each part of the code interacts, and once I can see it clearly, everything clicks into place for me.

Also sleeping, running or walking on a part of code I’m trying to understand is very helpful. I’ve solved a few codes right before going to sleep, the trouble is remembering it in the morning.

Reflect on how confident you feel using each of these problem-solving techniques/processes:

Technique/Process Reflection
Pseudocode I find it more engaging to imagine the code layout in my mind. Writing down little hints or keywords can sometimes help though.
Trying something Love this technique, I think the more you try and fail, the more the correct code becomes intuitive.
Rubber ducky method I think this technique will become one of favourites as I like verbally explaining the problem to myself, I will use this more in the future.
Reading error messages I don't always understand what it's throwing at me but, I like to try and if it fails, see why it's failing and go from there. (I like to start from the end and work back if that makes sense).
Console.logging Just like reading error messages, console.log is like a little window into the code, I find it helpful to have a response to what I’m doing.
Googling One of my favourite methods, I like to see other code and figure out how I can implement that code into mine.
Asking your peers for help I feel very comfortable doing this, as we are all here to learn, and generally, people love to help.
Asking coaches for help It's something I'm working on, and making an effort to do, I generally feel satifisfied solving the problem myself before reaching out.
Improving your process with reflection I think this the key to solifying the fundermentals, something I would like to improve on.

Reflect on a time you were reluctant to ask for help:

I first reached out for help because I deleted my .git repository from a blog file. I felt silly really because I should've known it was stored like that as we spent time learning all about git and repositories. I was stressed and couldn’t push anything up to GitHub, and was lost as my terminal window was saying all sorts of things. And then I thought, who cares, they’ve dealt with worse. It's probably easily fixable if I just reach out to someone who knows what they're doing. And it was. After that, I decided if I am struggling it only benefits me to reach out, no one really cares.
